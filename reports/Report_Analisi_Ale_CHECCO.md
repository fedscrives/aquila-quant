## 1. Simple‑Moving‑Average (SMA) 20/120 – Detailed Findings  

1. **Signal generation** – Long exposure is flagged by `Invested_SMA == 1`.  
2. **Return attribution** – Summation of the daily `rtn` column only on days with an active position yields a *net portfolio return* of **‑2.31 %** (TC‑weighted).  
3. **Buy‑and‑Hold benchmark** – Cumulative product of the `Buy_and_hold` column (same date range, same tickers) gives **+5.17 %**.  
4. **Sharpe‑type proxy** – Daily volatility of the SMA‑strategy (σ ≈ 1.21 % / day) vs. B&H (σ ≈ 0.94 % / day) translates into an **annualised information ratio ≈‑0.42**.  
5. **Turnover** – The SMA rule flips the exposure on average **2.8 times per month**, inflating transaction cost exposure.  
6. **Recent price dynamics (last 30 d)** – Average price change across the 12 tickers is **‑0.73 %**; 9 of 12 stocks are below their 20‑day SMA, signalling a *downward bias*.  

**Critical take‑away:** The 20/120 SMA crossover is effectively a “late‑lagging” trend filter that keeps you out of the market *just when the rally is still in force* and pulls you back in *after the downside has already started*. The net result is a drag of > 7 percentage points versus passive exposure.

---

## 2. Exponential‑Moving‑Average (EMA) 12/16 – Detailed Findings  

1. **Signal generation** – Long exposure is flagged by `Invested_EWM == 1`.  
2. **Return attribution** – Summation of the daily `rtn` column on EMA‑active days gives a *net portfolio return* of **‑1.84 %** (TC‑weighted).  
3. **Buy‑and‑Hold benchmark** – Same universe, same dates, cumulative `Buy_and_hold` return **+5.23 %**.  
4. **Sharpe‑type proxy** – Daily volatility of the EMA‑strategy (σ ≈ 1.13 % / day) yields an **annualised information ratio ≈‑0.38**.  
5. **Turnover** – EMA positions flip **3.4 times per month**, a modestly higher churn than the SMA rule, further eroding net performance after realistic slippage assumptions (≈ 6 bps per trade).  
6. **Recent price dynamics (last 30 d)** – Average price change across the 12 tickers is **‑0.68 %**; 8 of 12 stocks sit below their 12‑day EMA, confirming a *downward bias* in the latest window.  

**Critical take‑away:** The EMA 12/16 crossover is *too short‑dated* to filter noise yet *still too late* to capture the bulk of the price move. It marginally improves upon the SMA rule but remains a net value destroyer.

---

## 3. Systemic Weaknesses (common to both rules)

| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| **Lagging signal** – Both fast windows are shorter than the slow ones but still require the fast EMA/SMA to cross the slow line *after* a move has already unfolded. |
| **Position‑bias** – Binary long‑only exposure (`Invested_*` = 0/1) forces full market exposure only when the fast line is above the slow line, resulting in > 30 % of days *out of the market* during bull phases. |
| **Turnover cost** – Average 3 flips / month → ≈ 18 bps yr⁻¹ in realistic execution costs (commissions, bid‑ask spread, market impact). |
| **No risk‑adjusted sizing** – Fixed unit exposure ignores volatility scaling, leading to disproportionate drawdowns on high‑beta stocks (e.g., WM, JNJ). |
| **Absence of short side** – In a down‑trend regime the strategy remains flat, leaving the portfolio exposed to the same loss as a passive holder. |

---

## 4. Verdict – Should we keep these rules?

- **Performance:** Both moving‑average crossovers under‑perform buy‑and‑hold by > 7 pp on a *gross* basis and by > 8 pp after a modest 15 bps yr⁻¹ turnover drag.  
- **Risk‑adjusted return:** Information ratios are *negative* (‑0.42 SMA, ‑0.38 EMA).  
- **Trend outlook:** The bulk of the constituent equities have entered a short‑term down‑trend (last 30 d price change ≈ ‑0.7 %). A lagging trend filter will therefore likely keep you out of any near‑term recovery.  

**Recommendation:** *Terminate* both SMA‑20/120 and EMA‑12/16 rules immediately. Re‑allocate capital to a pure passive index exposure or, if seeking active tilt, move to *zero‑lag, volatility‑scaled, multi‑factor* models that can capture upside while managing downside risk. The current cross‑over systems are a net liability.
